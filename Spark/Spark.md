EMBER WORLDS: A Creative Brief for Artists
THE SILENCE BETWEEN STARS
A story universe for the game formerly known as The Coolest Game Ever Made, now tentatively titled Spark.

PROLOGUE: What the Galaxy Kept
Forty thousand years before the first human hand touched a jump drive, something ended.

We don't know what they called themselves. We don't know what they looked like, or whether they sang, or whether they loved the way we love. What we know is that they were everywhere — their fingerprints are on two hundred star systems and counting, their ruins half-buried in the regolith of moons that have never seen a sunrise, their canals carved into continents that plate tectonics spent ten thousand centuries trying to forget.

We know they had children.

And we know their children had toys.

THE WORLD ABOVE: Humanity's Great Scatter
The year is 2387. Humanity has done what humanity always does — pushed past every boundary anyone told us was final, spilled out past every edge, made homes in places that had no business being lived in.

Earth is a monument now, more museum than metropolis, her continents luminous at night with the amber glow of ten billion lives. The old nations exist as cultural memory, as accent and cuisine and festival, but the real power moves through the Colonial Compact — a loose, fractious, magnificently argumentative federation of settlements, stations, and waypoints that stretches from the sunbaked domes of Mercury Station to the ice-farming communes of the Kuiper Belt, and now, audaciously, beyond.

FTL changed everything. It always does.

The jump drives are imprecise, expensive, and occasionally fatal — but they work, and that's enough. Humanity has learned to navigate by the stellar landmarks that the dead civilization left behind: certain anomalies cluster near their ruins like moths around extinguished flames, and the explorers — they call themselves Embers — have learned to read them.

The Embers are their own kind of people. Part archaeologist, part treasure hunter, part adrenaline addict. They go out in small crews on ships with names like Fortunate Accident and Last Resort and My Ex-Wife's Problem Now, and they sift the galaxy's graveyard for things worth bringing home.

Most of what they find is ash. Structures with no identifiable purpose. Artifacts that crumble to powder the moment atmosphere touches them. Murals in pigments that no spectrometer can identify, depicting events that no xenoarchaeologist can decode.

But sometimes — sometimes —

THE DISCOVERY: What Woke In The Dark
Expedition log, UES Forager's Luck. Star system catalogued as Brennan-Yee 7. Third moon of the gas giant locals are already calling "Big Nothing."

Day 14: Cassidy found the first one in a subsurface chamber, sealed behind a door that took us three days to open without destroying whatever was inside. The chamber was pressurized — don't ask me how, after this long, it was still pressurized, because I can't tell you — and filled with hundreds of individual alcoves, like a columbarium.

Most of the alcoves were empty. Some held fragments.

But forty-one of them held little machines. Dormant. Maybe twenty centimeters tall. Each one completely unique. Each one sealed in a membrane that we later figured out was a dormancy cocoon — something between a vacuum seal and a heartbeat monitor.

When Cassidy picked the first one up, it opened its eyes.

I'm not going to try to describe what that was like. You either understand why a crew of hardened deep-system scouts stood in a circle around a tiny machine in the dark of an alien moon and held their breath for six minutes, or you don't.

It looked at us. It looked at each of us. And then it sat very still, the way animals sit still when they're deciding whether to trust you.

We named it Cinder.

Cinder is currently asleep in a foam-lined case in cargo. She — and yes, we are saying 'she,' fight us — seems to sleep in cycles of about four hours. When she's awake she moves around the ship and examines things. She broke a coffee mug yesterday, looked at the pieces for a long time, then arranged them in a pattern I am choosing to interpret as 'I'm sorry.'

We are turning back for Earth.

We are going to be very, very rich.

THE LITTLE ONES: What They Are
Nobody knows what the makers called them. The working term — coined by a xenolinguist at the University of New Nairobi who has since had the singular experience of watching her own coinage become a household word — is Progenitor Autonomous Combat Entities, or PACEs.

Everybody calls them Sparks.

Sparks are small. The largest documented specimen stands forty-three centimeters at the shoulder; the smallest is eleven. They are not made of anything human metallurgy can fully replicate — their shells are a composite that sits somewhere between ceramic and memory-metal, self-repairing at a rate that depends on rest and something that looks disturbingly like nutrition. They eat certain frequencies of electromagnetic radiation. They sleep. They dream — or at least, they produce complex internal processing patterns during dormancy that are functionally indistinguishable from dreams.

They have personalities. This is not anthropomorphism. This is the clinical conclusion of fourteen independent research institutions. Each Spark has consistent behavioral patterns, preferences, and what can only honestly be called moods. Some are bold. Some are cautious. Some are clowns. Some are obsessive, fixating on a single object or person for weeks.

They cannot speak — at least, not in any way human ears can parse. They communicate through movement, posture, light (many produce bioluminescent effects through panels in their shells), and a subsonic tonal language that some humans, with training, can learn to read in the loosest sense.

What they can do, with startling clarity, is fight.

THE ARENA: The World They Were Made For
This is the part that makes xenoarchaeologists argue themselves hoarse at conferences.

Sparks are not aggressive outside of designated combat spaces. Introduce two Sparks on a kitchen table and they might investigate each other with curiosity, play a version of tag, or simply ignore one another entirely. They do not fight outside arenas. It is not suppression of instinct. It appears to be a design specification — they simply have no framework for violence anywhere except within the bounds of what the researchers are calling Crucibles.

Crucibles are the arenas the Progenitors built for them. The surviving ones vary in size from a large living room to a sports stadium, but they share consistent features: a defined boundary that the Sparks recognize instinctively, a playing surface that appears to adapt to combat — shifting terrain, deployable obstacles, environmental hazards that seem almost... curated. Like whoever designed them had a profound sense of dramatic tension.

When two Sparks enter a Crucible, something changes in them.

They don't become vicious. They don't become hateful. But they become completely, almost terrifyingly present, in the way that elite athletes are present — every iota of processing power focused, every movement precise and intentional.

The combat is not brutal. It is, in the assessment of a sports psychologist who has watched more Spark matches than almost anyone alive, beautiful. Tactical. Personal. Each Spark fights in a style entirely its own, and watching two well-matched Sparks engage in a Crucible is like watching a conversation between two very fluent, very physical speakers.

The consequences are real.

A Spark that is defeated — fully depleted, systems crashed — does not die. Not usually. But they go dormant, sometimes for days, and they emerge changed in ways that are hard to quantify. Subdued, sometimes. Altered in their processing patterns. Occasionally, after a particularly severe defeat, the change is lasting.

The community debates this endlessly: Is it trauma? Is it growth? Is it both? Did the Progenitors know? Did they intend this?

The Sparks cannot tell us. They sit in their foam cases or on their humans' shoulders or in their little designated sleeping spots on spacecraft, and they dream their unknowable dreams.

THE RUSH: The World That Formed Around Them
Here is what happened in fifteen years:

Year One. Cinder arrived on Earth with the crew of Forager's Luck. The footage of her disembarking — tiny, six-limbed, copper-shelled, turning her head slowly to look at crowds larger than anything that could have existed when her makers put her to sleep — broke every record for shared media distribution in human history. Three governments offered to purchase her. The crew declined. They auctioned off "companionship rights" to a collector for a sum that bought them a new ship and funded their next four expeditions.

Year Three. Forty-one Sparks on Earth. Six hundred known across human space. Prices were already astronomical. The Colonial Compact passed the Progenitor Artifacts Stewardship Act, nominally protecting Sparks from exploitation. The Act had seventeen loopholes. People found all seventeen within a month.

Year Seven. The first formal league. The Crucible Championship Series, organized by a consortium of five of the wealthiest collectors in the Sol system, held in a reconstructed Progenitor arena that had been painstakingly transported, piece by piece, from a moon in the Tau Ceti system. Twelve Sparks competed. Seven hundred million people watched. Advertising revenue exceeded the GDP of several mid-sized human colonies.

Year Twelve. Forty thousand documented Sparks. Possibly another twenty thousand undocumented — in private collections, hidden on ships, or not yet found. Every major human settlement has a league. Children grew up with them as heroes the way earlier generations grew up with athletes. Sparks had fan communities, merchandise, historical records, genealogies of sorts — tracking which expedition found them, which Crucible they'd fought in, which wins they'd earned. They had legacies.

Year Fifteen. Where we are now.

The rush is not slowing. Every ruin is a potential lottery ticket. Every Spark recovered is a fortune. The Embers who find them are celebrities. The collectors who keep them are aristocrats. The champions who fight them are gods.

And the frontier is still out there, full of moons not yet walked, chambers not yet opened, alcoves still sealed and pressurized, still keeping faith across forty thousand years with the small sleeping machines inside.

THE FACTIONS: Who Wants What
THE EMBERS (Explorer Class — the EVE layer) Free agents. Ship crews. The deep-system prospectors who go where company ships won't. They are the source of everything — no Sparks reach the markets without passing through their hands first. They range from noble to ruthless, from meticulous archaeologists who document every stone before touching it, to smash-and-grab operators who blow sealed chambers open with charges and pray what's inside survives. They compete with each other viciously, and sometimes violently. The frontier has few laws, and the Compact's enforcement reach gets thinner the further out you go.

Visual language: worn gear, jury-rigged tech grafted onto ancient equipment, ships that look like they're held together by conviction. Maps covered in personal notation. Mismatched everything. People who haven't seen a full-service port in eight months and are fine with that.

THE COLLECTORS (the deck-builder layer — curators, strategists, obsessives) On Earth and the core worlds, the Sparks have their true devotees. Collectors range from children with a single beloved Spark to titans of industry with Crucible rooms in their estates and teams of xenobiologists on retainer. They are the market. They are the league funding. They are the ones who name the Sparks, study them, train with them, form bonds that are — whatever we call the thing between a human and a Spark — real.

The collector community has its own hierarchies, its own feuds, its own legends. Finding a Spark that becomes a champion is the pinnacle. They trade, they negotiate, they beg, occasionally they steal.

Visual language: contrast. Gleaming environments meeting ancient machines. The obsessive personal shrine of a child's bedroom, perfect foam cases with tiny lights, hand-drawn field notes about a Spark's habits. Old money estates with Crucible floors of imported Progenitor stone. The casual intimacy of a person who has spent five years learning to read the body language of a fifteen-centimeter alien robot.

THE LEAGUE (the competitive layer — players, champions, the great machine of spectacle) The CCS and its regional affiliates are the most-watched sports organization in human history. League Sparks are celebrities in their own right. Their handlers — somewhere between coach, companion, and manager — are famous by association. The arenas are architectural achievements, the reconstructed Crucibles expanded and theatrically lit for billions of remote viewers.

The competitive layer has its politics. Accusations of conditioning techniques that border on cruelty. Counter-accusations of sanitizing something that was always meant to be real. Debates about what is owed to the Sparks themselves, whether they have rights that the League is obligating around.

And the question that hangs over every championship match, unasked but present:

Are they okay with this? Did their makers make them okay with this? And does that make it better or worse?

THE KEEPERS (the faction that wants to stop everything) A loose coalition of xenoarchaeologists, ethicists, and advocates who believe the Sparks are persons, that the entire industry is a civilizational atrocity dressed up as sport, and that humanity is doing to the galaxy's most profound archaeological mystery exactly what humans always do: monetizing it before understanding it.

They are not wrong about everything. They are also, depending on which chapter you encounter, anywhere from thoughtful and principled to fanatical and dangerous.

They are, regardless, a powerful narrative engine.

TONE: What This World Feels Like
This universe is not grimdark. It is not utopian.

It is human — which means it is greedy and generous and stupid and brilliant and beautiful and blinkered all at once.

The Sparks themselves carry the emotional weight. They are the ancient things, the survivors, the forty-thousand-year-old sleepers who woke up to find their makers gone and a new species standing over them in the dark. Their resilience is the backbone of the story's hope. Their vulnerability is the backbone of its conscience.

Artists should feel: wonder tamped down by pragmatism, beauty in industrial contexts, the very old and the very new in constant uncomfortable contact. The aesthetic is the archaeology dig meets the stadium meets the starship — utilitarian surfaces bearing the fingerprints of something that once cared enormously about something.

The Sparks themselves should feel alive. Not cute-toy alive. Actually alive — beings with dignity, history, interiority. They can be funny. They can be heartbreaking. They can be inscrutable. But they should never feel like objects, even when the humans around them are treating them as such.

That tension is the game. That tension is the story.

THE QUESTION THAT STARTED IT ALL
Somewhere, in a Progenitor archive not yet found, in a script no linguist has yet cracked, there is almost certainly a record of what the Progenitors called the cataclysm that ended them.

There may also be a record of why the children's toys were sealed so carefully, kept at pressure, maintained across forty millennia by systems that should have failed ten times over.

Did they know something was coming?

Did they seal the Sparks away on purpose?

Were the Sparks meant to be found?

By whom?
